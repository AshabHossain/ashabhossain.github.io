Featured Projects

Project 01 — Instrumentation & ML

Smart Temperature Calibration System

Calibrating a thermocouple (a temperature sensor) normally means a person sits in front of a furnace for hours, manually writing down readings. I built a system that does this entire job automatically.

A phone camera watches the furnace display, and a machine-learning model reads the numbers from the screen — just like a human would, but without errors or fatigue. At the same time, an Arduino board records the sensor's own reading. The software merges both readings, plots a calibration curve, and tells you exactly how accurate the sensor is — all hands-free.

Project 02 — Thermal Systems

Portable Climate Control Box

Imagine a small, affordable box that can cool or heat a tiny space — like a mini air conditioner and heater in one, without any refrigerant gas. That's what I designed and built from scratch.

It uses a special chip called a Peltier module — one side gets hot, the other gets cold when electricity flows through it. I used this principle to build two units: one that controls temperature (ranging from cool 19°C to warm 50°C), and another that removes moisture from the air by condensing it on a cold copper tube. The whole thing was designed in 3D software, hand-fabricated, and cost under $80.

TEC-12706 Peltier SolidWorks Closed-Loop Cooling Copper Condenser Digital Sensors DC Pump
Project 03 — Structural Engineering

Will This Crane Hook Break? A Virtual Stress Test

Before a real crane hook is used on a construction site, engineers need to know: at what weight will it fail? Instead of breaking real hooks (which is expensive and dangerous), I tested one entirely inside a computer using simulation software.

I modelled a complete lifting system — hook, rope, beam, and supporting column — then applied heavy loads to see where stress builds up and whether the metal would crack or bend. The result? The hook is safe at lighter loads, but at twice the weight it's dangerously close to failure. I also predicted how many loading cycles it could survive before fatigue sets in, and recommended specific design changes to make it safer.

Research

B.Sc. Thesis — Dept. of Mechanical Engineering, BUET

Capability-Aware Autonomous Navigation for Torque-Adaptive Mobile Robots

Most robots treat every bump, slope, or rough patch as a wall they can't cross — so they waste time going around things they could actually drive over. My thesis asks: what if the robot knew its own strength? What if it could look at a rough surface and decide "I can handle that" or "that's too much for my wheels"? I built a robot that does exactly that.

Download Full Thesis

The Problem

Today's robots use navigation software that sees the world in black and white: flat floor = safe, everything else = obstacle. This means a robot will take a 40-second detour around a small gravel patch it could easily drive across in 8 seconds. It has no concept of "I'm strong enough to handle that."

What I Built

I designed a navigation system that gives the robot self-awareness about its own physical limits. Using a depth camera (like the sensors in a modern smartphone), the robot scans the ground ahead and classifies each patch as "easy to cross," "crossable but need more power," or "too risky." Based on this judgement, it picks the smartest route and automatically adjusts how hard each wheel pushes — more power on rough ground, less on smooth surfaces.

Key Results

  • The robot completed tasks 67% of the time in real-world tests — vs. 50% for the next-best approach
  • It reached goals 5× faster (8 seconds vs. 43 seconds) by driving through terrain instead of around it
  • It automatically applied 3–4× more wheel power on rough surfaces while keeping a steady speed
  • In 50 simulated test runs, it succeeded 88–91% of the time
